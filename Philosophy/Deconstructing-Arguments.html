Deconstructing Arguments

TL;DR

This note will discuss the foundational skill required in reading philosophical texts: Identifying its essential components. By the end, we will understand how to pinpoint the thesis (i.e. the conclusion, the premises (i.e. the reasons that support the thesis), and the overall argumentative structure of the text/passage.

What is an Argument?

Despite the common language ideas that we might have regarding what an “argument” is, philosophy has a very precise and technical definition for the term.

Philosophical Argument

An argument is a set of sentences such that one of them is being said to be true, and the other(s) are being offered as reasons for believing in the truth of the first.

A philosophical argument

It is Friday. Marianne always wears jeans on a Friday, so Marianne will be wearing jeans today. What are the sentence?

  • It is a Friday
  • Marianne always wears jeans on a Friday
  • Marianne will wear jeans today

Conclusion

The sentence that is said to be true in a philosophical argument.

Premises

The sentence(s) being offered as reason(s) for believing the conclusion of a philosophical argument.

For ease of writing, from now on:

There could be as many premises as the author of the argument requires.

In the previous example, the conclusion is Marianne will wear jeans today.

Inherent nature of a sentence

Note that our definitions of a conclusion or premise in an argument, as provided here, are purely functional roles assigned to a sentence. A sentence is not intrinsically a premise or a conclusion!

Standard Form

The so-called “Standard Form” is a representation of an argument that clarifies the structure and the role of the sentences in the argument. It looks something like this:

  • (P1) Premise 1
  • (P2) Premise 2
  • More Premises here
  • (C) Therefore, Conclusion

What is the point of a philosophical paper?

Philosophical papers, unlike textbooks or scientific papers, do not communicate factual information and do not deliver exposition. Rather, they make arguments and are intended to be persuasive, i.e. they aim to convince the reader to believe their conclusions.

What can a premise contain? It can consist of:

  • Factual information
  • Scientific data
  • Thought experiments - gendanken for all the physics (and German) people here
  • Conceptual truths
  • Counter-examples
  • etc.

The premises need to be either true OR false - thus commands, questions, and exclamations can not be premises because they do not have a binary truth value.

A deconstructed argument

  • (P1) To have a mental state is to have a disposition to behave in certain ways.
  • (P2) Pain is a mental state.
  • (C) Therefore, to be in pain is to have a disposition to behave in certain ways.

Distinguishing Arguments from Non-Arguments

A crucial part of analyzing a philosophical text is deciphering what is part of an argument, and what isn’t. While it would be ideal (and very boring) to have philosophical texts that comprise only of demarcated arguments one-after-the-other, for readability and flourish authors do add explanations, descriptions, asides, and assertions.

Argument vs. Causal Explanations

This is one of the most common points of confusion when trying to locate the arguments in a philosophical text. This is mostly because both an argument and a causal explanation use the word “because” - but they do so for different purposes.

Different uses of "because"

An argument uses a “rational because”. It provides reasons to believe that a conclusion is true.

Rational-because

“The streets are wet, because it has been raining”. Here, the rain is the reason for believing that the streets are wet.

A causal explanation uses a “causal because”. It states the cause of an accepted fact.

Causal-because

“The storm broke because the clouds formed and the sky blackened”. This is not an argument for why the storm broke, but it is an explanation of the physical causes for the storm to break.

This is not that alien from what is done in science - there too, we constantly shuffle between trying to seek logical justification and identifying causal mecahnisms.
