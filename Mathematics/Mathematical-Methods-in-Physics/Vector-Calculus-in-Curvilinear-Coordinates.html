      Vector Calculus in Curvilinear Coordinates

      Vector Calculus in Curvilinear Coordinates

      Mar 30, 20259 min read

      • curvilinear
      • vector
      • calculus
      • mathematics

      We will going over the three fundamental theorems of vector calculus in generalised curvilinear coordinates.

      Notation

      We identify a point in space by some 3-tuple of its “coordinates” - (u,v,w). We assume that the system is based on some orthogonal basis, i.e. the unit vectors u^,v^,w^ are pointing in mutually orthogonal directions.

      Note

      In general curvilinear orthogonal coordinates, the unit vector are supposed to be functions of the coordinates themselves. This is due to the fact that unlike the Cartesian system (which also qualifies as a curvilinear orthogonal coordinate system), it is not guaranteed that the unit vectors will point in the same direction at all points

      Now, any 3D vector can be expressed using its coordinates in this vector space. In particular, we may attempt to represent the infinitesimal displacement vector from (u,v,w) to (u+du,v+dv,w+dw) as:

      dl=fduu^+gdvv^+hdww^

      where f,g,h are some functions of coordinates, and a characteristic of the coordinate system that we choose - i.e. f=g=h=1 for Cartesian coordinate system. These three functions can tell you all that you need to know about a particular coordinate system.

      Gradient

      Let t(u,v,w) be a scalar function of the coordinates in the space we just described. If we carry out an infinitesimal change in coordinates from (u,v,w)→(u+du,+dv,w+dw) , t will change by an amount given by:

      dt=∂u∂t​du+∂v∂t​dv+∂w∂t​dw

      as a standard result from the definition of partial derivatives. We can represent this as a dot product, given by:

      dt≡∇t⋅dl=(∇t)u​fdu+(∇t)v​gdv+(∇t)w​hdw

      To make this agree with the partial derivative result, we have to adopt the following definitions:

      (∇t)u​=f1​∂u∂t​,(∇t)v​=g1​∂v∂t​,(∇t)w​=h1​∂w∂t​

      as can be seen by a simple substitution.

      Thus, we may define:

      Gradient

      The gradient of a scalar function of coordinates in a vector space is given by:

      ∇t≡f1​∂u∂r​u^+g1​∂v∂t​v^+h1​∂w∂t​w^

      If we know the functions f,g,h for a particular coordinate system, we can easily construct the gradient.

      Fundamental theorem of gradients

      From the equation that defines dt using the gradient, we can see that a total change in t going from a point a to a point b is given by:

      t(b)−t(a)=∫ab​dt=∫ab​(∇t)⋅dl

      This result is a case of a clear substitution combined with the fundamental theorem of calculus, so there is nothing to prove here in this context.

      Divergence

      Now, let us a have a vector function of the coordinates, given by:

      A(u,v,w)=Au​(u,v,w)u^+Av​(u,v,w)v^+Aw​(u,v,w)w^

      Where Au​,Av​,Aw​ are component scalar functions. A common thing to do with such a function is the find the integral ∮A⋅da over some surface. In general, the surface might look like this:

      (Source: Griffiths, Introduction to Electromagnetism - Figure A.2)

      Note that the surface has been generated by incrementing the coordinates infinitesimally, in sequence. The sides of this “rectangular” solid is given by dlu​=fdu, dlv​=gdv, dlw​=hdw. Thus, the volume of the solid is given by:

      dτ=dlu​dlv​dlw​=(fgh)dudvdw

      Now, we find the components of A⋅da on each face - for the “front” face, we have:

      da=−(gh)dvdwu^

      and thus,

      A⋅da=−(ghAu​)dvdw

      The back surface is identical to this one, and just differs in the sign. But we must note that in the case of the back surface, the function is to be evaluated at u+du instead of u. For any differentiable function, we have:

      F(u+du)−F(u)=dudf​du

      taking du to be infinitesimal. We note that due to the sign flipping on the back and the front surface, combined with the different values of u at which the function is evaluated, we have the RHS of the above equation in terms of Au​. Adding the contributions from the front and back surfaces, we thus get:

      [∂u∂​(ghAu​)]dudvdw=fgh1​∂u∂​(ghAu​)dτ

      Carrying out the same procedure for the top-bottom and left-right surface pairs, we get:

      ∮A⋅da=fgh1​[∂u∂​(ghAu​)+∂v∂​(fhAv​)+∂w∂​(fgAw​)]dτ

      The coefficient of dτ serves the role of specifying the divergence of A in the given curvilinear coordinates. Thus, we may write:

      ∇⋅A≡fgh1​[∂u∂​(ghAu​)+∂v∂​(fhAv​)+∂w∂​(fgAw​)]

      and we may rewrite the surface integral as:

      ∮A⋅da=(∇⋅A)dτ

      Note: this does not prove the divergence theorem, as it says something about infinitesimal volumes only! It is reasonable to assume that a finite solid can be split into many infinitesimal solids, and thus the relation holds - but that is not the case, as the LHS of the equation in that case would have terms that contribute to not just the outside surface of the finite solid but also many more surfaces. Luckily, we can see that da always points outwards, and any surface that is not a part of the external surface of the finite solid will necessarily have to be facing another surface with a da pointing in the opposite direction. Thus, all the extra contributions cancel out neatly, and we are left with the result of the divergence theorem:

      Divergence Theorem

      For a vector function A, and finite volume V bounded by a closed surface S, we have:

      ∮S​A⋅da=∫V​(∇⋅A)dτ

      (Note that this holds for any region V - a more detailed proof is omitted here)

      Curl

      To arrive at the form of curl, we have to look at the line integral:

      ∮A⋅dl

      around an infinitesimal loop generated by starting at (u,v,w) and successively increasing u and v, all the while holding w constant. We imagine this surface (again, in the infinitesimal limit) to be a rectangle with the dimensions:

      dlu​=fdu,dlv​=gdv

      Therefore, the area of the loop is given by:

      da=(fg)dudv

      We make an assumption: the coordinate system is right-handed. Thus, w^ points out of the page in this figure. We choose this as the positive direction of da, and we follow the anti-clockwise traversal of the loop as shown.

      We look at each segment of the loop. Along the bottom segment, we have:

      dl=fduu^

      and thus, we get,

      A⋅dl=(fAu​)du

      On the top, the sign is reversed for the dl component and A is not evaluated at (v+dv) instead of v. Adding the contributions of this top-bottom pairs, we get:

      [−(fAu​)∣v+dv​+(fAu​)∣v​]du=−[∂v∂​(fAu​)]dudv

      Carrying out the same manipulation on the right-left segment pair, we get:

      [∂u∂​(gAv​)]dudv

      Combing the terms,

      ∮A⋅dl=[∂u∂​(gAv​)−∂v∂​(fAu​)]dudv=fg1​[∂u∂​(gAv​)−∂v∂​(fAu​)]w^⋅da

      The coefficient of da on the RHS gives us the w^ component of the curl. Constructing the u^,v^ components using the same method, we arrive at a definition for the curl:

      ∇×A=gh1​[∂v∂​(hAw​)−∂w∂​(gAv​)]u^+fh1​[∂w∂​(fAu​)−∂u∂​(hAw​)]v^+fg1​[∂u∂​(gAv​)−∂v∂​(fAu​)]w^

      Giving us the familiar result:

      ∮A⋅dl=(∇×A)⋅da

      This is not yet Stoke’s theorem, as it says something about an infinitesimal loop only. Like in the case of the divergence theorem, in this case too we can argue that any finite loop can be chopped in to infinitesimal loops such that all segments of the loop apart from the segments at the boundaries cancel each other out by virtue of being traversed in opposite directions for loop touching each other. This give us the Stoke’s theorem,

      Stokes' Theorem

      For a finite loop L that closes a surface of area s, we have:

      ∮L​A⋅dl=∫S​(∇×A)⋅da

      Laplacian

      The form of this operator for the generalised curvilinear coordinate system can be derived from the fact that is a scalar, and the divergence of a gradient. Using the previously derived formulae for both of those operations, we derive the Laplacian as,

      ∇2t=fgh1​[∂u∂​(fgh​∂u∂t​)+∂v∂​(gfh​∂v∂t​)+∂w∂​(hfg​∂w∂t​)]
