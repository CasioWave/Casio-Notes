      Infinite Series

      Infinite Series

      May 20, 202516 min read

      • mathematics
      • preliminaries

      Infinite series are the mostly widely used tools in physics, and it is crucial to know how to use them properly. The topics discussed here serve as preludes to further analysis, or numerical methods.

      Note that this will not be a rigorous exposition on infinite series - that belongs to the realm of mathematical analysis. We will be satisfied by just knowing the tricks of the trade.

      Fundamental definitions

      Usually, we go about defining an infinite series using something called partial sums.

      Partial Sums

      Let us have a sequence {an​}n=1∞​. Then, we define the ith partial sum of this sequence as:

      si​=n=1∑i​an​

      This is a finite sum, and thus offers no problem. A convenient starting point.

      Now, we define,

      n→∞lim​sn​=n=1∑∞​an​

      as the infinite series.

      We can now comment on the “convergence” of such a sum.

      What is the criteria for convergence of an infinite series?

      1. First Principle Criteria: If the limit limn→∞​sn​ exists, and is equal to some S, then we say that the infinite series converges, and is equal to S. This is denoted by ∑n=1∞​an​=S. Note that is a definition of the value of the series - one that is consistent with our ideas of addition.
      2. Cauchy Criteria: If ∀ϵ>0 ∃Nϵ​∈N such that ∣si​−sj​∣<ϵ ∀i,j>Nϵ​, then we say that the series is Cauchy, and that it converges. This essentially means that the partial sums are “bunching together” for sufficiently large values of n.

      There are mainly three types of series:

      1. Convergent Series: Satisfying the definition of convergence defined above.
      2. Divergent Series: Series that do not converge.
      3. Oscillatory Series: Series that do not converge to a value, but oscillate between two bounds in the sufficiently large n limit.

      Example: Oscillatory Series

      The series defined by,

      n=1∑∞​un​=1+(−1)+1+⋯+(−1)n+…

      oscillates between 1 and 0 in the limit n→∞.

      It should be noted that often Oscillatory Series are subsumed under Divergent Series, i.e. all Oscillatory series are divergent series but not all divergent series are oscillatory series.

      Note: The requirement that limn→∞​an​=0 for ∑n=0∞​an​ to converge is a necessary requirement, but not a sufficient one. This means that all convergent series satisfy this necessary condition, but not all series satisfying the necessary condition are convergent!

      Geometric Series

      Let us define a sequence of terms,

      an​=a0​rn

      Where a0​,r∈R, and n∈N. r is obviously the ratio of successive terms, and the series looks like,

      n=0∑∞​an​=a0​+a0​r+a0​r2+…

      The nth partial sum of the series has a convenient closed form given by,

      sn​=a0​1−r1−rn​

      How do we arrive at this expression?

      Just multiply LHS and RHS of the definition of the nth partial sum with r, and then subtract from the original defining equation of the nth partial sum. Only two terms survive on the RHS, and you can divide out by a factor of 1−r to get this expression.

      Does it converge?

      If we restrict to ∣r∣<1, we may easily compute the limit of the nth partial sum as,

      n→∞lim​sn​=n→∞lim​a0​1−r1−rn​=1−ra0​​

      Which is a finite value. Thus, the geometric series converges.

      The Harmonic Series

      We define a sequence of terms as,

      an​=n1​

      Where n∈N. The series looks like,

      n=1∑∞​an​=11​+21​+31​+⋯+n1​+…

      Does it converge?

      We note that the Harmonic Series satisfies the necessary condition for convergence, as limn→∞​n1​=0. Now, let us group the terms in the sum together:

      (1+21​)+(31​+41​)+(51​+61​+71​+81​)+(91​+⋯+161​)+…

      Note that each bracketed sum is of the form: (ignore 1+21​, it is a minor inconsistency and does not affect the limits)

      p+11​+p+21​+⋯+p+p1​

      Of course, each of these p terms are greater than 2p1​. This gives us the lower bound:

      p+11​+p+21​+⋯+p+p1​>2pp​=21​

      So, we have a sum of these brackets, each bracket greater than 21​. This leads us to conclude that the harmonic series diverges.

      It is very important to keep track of the series that we have classified as convergent or divergent, as they can then be used in several tests for convergence that we have derived.

      Comparison Tests

      Let us have a series defined by the sequence {an​}n=1∞​ (that we want to test for convergence), and sequence {bn​}n=1∞​ whose series is known to be convergent. Then, if the former satisfies,

      0≤an​≤bn​,∀n>N∈N

      then we can conclude that the series defined by ∑n=1∞​an​ is convergent.

      Why does this work?

      As a rough motivation, we can see that using the Cauchy criteria for convergence ∣si​−sj​∣=∑n=j+1i​an​, which is smaller than the corresponding quantity for the converging series by definition - for a sufficiently large n limit. This allows us to say that the series converges.

      Note that the converse is also true, i.e. if ∑n=1∞​bn​ diverges and the series in question satisfies,

      0≤bn​≤an​

      then ∑n=1∞​an​ also diverges.

      Tip

      Here we realise why classifying some standard series like the Geometric and Harmonic series as convergent or divergent serves a useful purpose - it ensures that we can use them in tests like the comparison test to see whether other series are convergent or divergent.

      Comparison Test for a divergent series

      Let us define a series for the sequence an​=n−p where p=0.999. We know that for n∈N, we have,

      n−0.999≥n−1

      and that bn​=n−1 are the terms for the Harmonic series - which diverges. Thus, by the comparison test, we can state that ∑n=1∞​an​ diverges.

      Cauchy Root Test

      Let us take a series ∑n=1∞​an​. If the terms of this series satisfy (for sufficiently large n),

      (an​)(1/n)≤r<1

      Where r∈R is a number independent of n, then we can say that the series is convergent. Conversely, if we have,

      (an​)(1/n)≥1

      for sufficiently large n, then we can say that the series diverges.

      Why does this work?

      This is essentially a special case of the comparison test that makes application easier for power series (note that an1/n​ is often easy to compute for the terms of a power series). This can be seen by raising the inequality to the nth power,

      an​≤rn<1

      Which tells us that the terms of the series in question are always smaller than the corresponding term from a convergent Geometric series! By comparison test, the test holds.

      D’Alembert (or Cauchy) Ratio Test

      Let us take a series ∑n=1∞​an​. For sufficiently large n, if we have,

      an​an+1​​≤r<1

      Where r∈R is a number independent of n, then we say that the series converges. Conversely, if for sufficiently large n we have,

      an​an+1​​>1

      we can conclude that the series in divergent.

      Alternate statement: If we have a series ∑n=1∞​an​ such that,

      1. limn→∞​an​an+1​​<1: The Series converges
      2. limn→∞​an​an+1​​>1: The Series diverges
      3. limn→∞​an​an+1​​=1: The test is inconclusive.

      This test is not as sensitive as the root test (owing to the indeterminacy), but it is very easy to apply. If it fails, we can always move on to a more sensitive test.

      Why does the indeterminacy appear?

      The reason is present in the initial statement of the test,

      an​an+1​​≤r<1

      for sufficiently large n. Note that we may be able to find that an​an+1​​<1 for all finite n, but we not be able to find a r<1 that is independent of n such that an​an+1​​≤r.

      An example of this indeterminacy

      Let us look at the Harmonic Series. Here, an​an+1​​=n+1n​ Which is always smaller than 1 and larger than 21​ ∀n∈N. But, it is not possible to find an r that is independent of n, such that it serves as an upper bound for the ratio an​an+1​​ for sufficiently large n. Thus, we call the test indeterminate in this case.

      Cauchy (or Maclaurin) Integral Test

      In this test, we compare the series with an integral - i.e. the sum of the area of rectangles of unit width and height equal to terms of the series, with area under the curve of a function.

      Let f(x) be a continuous, monotonically decreasing function, such that f(n)=an​. Then, the series ∑n=1∞​an​ converges if the integral ∫1∞​f(x)dx is finite, and diverges if the integral is infinite.

      Why does this work?

      Note that the partial sums are,

      si​=n=1∑i​an​=n=1∑i​f(n)

      Because the function f(x) is monotonically decreasing, we can always write two inequalities:

      si​≥∫1i+1​f(x)dx

      and

      si​−a1​≤∫1i​f(x)dx

      Graphically, this is obvious - as seen below.

      From this, we get,

      ∫1∞​f(x)d≤n=1∑∞​an​≤∫1∞​f(x)d+a1​

      i.e. a classical limit sandwich. If the integral has a finite value, the series must converge, and if the integral diverges the series must also diverge!

      A particular use for the Integral test

      It is very useful for setting lower and upper bounds on the remaining terms of a series when some of the series has been summed up.

      n=1∑∞​an​=n=1∑N​an​+n=N+1∑∞​an​

      We can now use those two inequalities mentioned before 1 to write,

      ∫N+1∞​f(x)d≤n=N+1∑∞​an​≤∫N+1∞​f(x)d+aN+1​

      which give us upper and lower bounds as required.

      Reducing the requirements on f(x)

      We previously required the function f(x) to be positive and monotonic - which are quite restrictive requirements. Now, if f(x) is a function with a continuous derivative, then we have,

      n=N1​∑N2​​f(n)=∫N1​N2​​f(x)dx+∫N1​N2​​(x−⌊x⌋)f′(x)dx(1)

      Here, ⌊x⌋ represents the “floor” of x, i.e. the largest integer smaller or equal to x, and f′(x) is the derivative of f with respect to x. Note that x−⌊x⌋={x}, or the fractional part of x. It varies like a saw-tooth, rising linearly from an integer to the next and then dropping abruptly to zero.

      Why is this relevant? If both of the integrals in this equation converge, then the infinite series also converges. If exactly one of the integrals diverges, then the series diverges. If both of the integrals diverge, we have a tricky situation - the test is indeterminate unless it can be shown that the divergences of the integrals “cancel out”.

      How do we arrive at those integrals?

      Let us reason backwards, and show that RHS is equal to LHS.

      1. Using integration by parts, we see that ∫N1​N2​​xf′(x)dx=N2​f(N2​)−N1​f(N1​)−∫N1​N2​​f(x)dx.
      2. On the floor function integral, we see, ∫N1​N2​​⌊x⌋f′(x)dx=∑n=N1​N2​−1​n∫nn+1​f′(x)dx=∑n=N1​N2​−1​n[f(n+1)−f(n)]. This final expression simplifies into,
      −n=N1​+1∑N2​​f(n)−N1​f(N1​)+N2​f(N2​)

      Adding them together, we see that everything cancels out nicely and we are left with the original result.

      Alternatively, we can also use the integral “decomposition”:

      n=N1​+1∑N2​​f(n)=∫N1​N2​​f(x)dx+∫N1​N2​​(x−⌊x⌋−21​)f′(x)dx+21​[f(N2​)−f(N1​)](2)

      This one has the added benefit of being symmetrical about zero (the saw tooth has been shifted). We may derive it in the same method as the one before.

      Equations (1) and (2) can be used in alternating series, and those with irregular sign sequences owing to the lack of monotonicity requirement.

      Riemann Zeta Function

      We define the Riemann Zeta Functions as,

      ζ(p)=n=1∑∞​n−p

      Let us use the integral test that we just developed on this.

      ∫1∞​x−pdx=−p+1x−p+1​∣x=1∞​,p=1 or ln(x)∣x=1∞​,p=1

      The integral is divergent for p≤1 and is convergent for p>1 - this is an independent proof that the Harmonic series diverges logarithmically (which means it diverges very slowly, like a logarithm).

      Euler Mascheroni Constant

      Interestingly, we can define a special constant using the fact that while neither the Harmonic series nor ln converge as expected, their difference does!

      γ=n→∞lim​(m=1∑n​m−1−ln(n))

      Which is known as the Euler-Mascheroni constant.

      A slowly diverging function

      Consider,

      n=2∑∞​nln(n)1​

      Clearly, the denominator here grows just a tiny bit faster than when it was just the n. Using the integral test on it,

      ∫2∞​xln(x)1​dx=ln(ln(x))∣x=2∞​

      Which is divergent, meaning that the series is also divergent. This series diverges even slower than the Harmonic series (which already diverges very slowly - the sum of the first million terms or so of the Harmonic series is less than 15).

      More sensitive tests

      There are more tests, some of which are more sensitive than the ones discussed before. Many of these tests are a result of Kummer’s Theorem.

      Kummer’s Theorem

      Kummer's Theorem

      Let an​ and un​ define two series of finite positive terms. Then, we can say, The series ∑n​un​ converges if

      n→∞lim​(an​un+1​un​​−an+1​)≥C>0

      Where C is some constant. Note that:

      1. This is equivalent to the comparison test if the series ∑n​an−1​ converges. In that case, there is no new information to be found here.
      2. This gives us new information if the series ∑n​an−1​ diverges. The more weakly it diverges, the stronger is the test. Also, if ∑n​an−1​ diverges, and
      n→∞lim​(an​un+1​un​​−an+1​)≤0

      then ∑n​un​ diverges.

      Proof

      The second part of the theorem follows directly from the comparison test - in this case between ∑n​an−1​ as the known diverging series and ∑n​un​ as the series being tested. This can be seen by a simple rearrangement that gives us an inequality between the ratios of successive terms of both the series. To prove the first part, we first try to write down the cases for n=N+1 to any larger n.

      uN+1​≤CaN​uN​−aN+1​uN+1​​

      thus,

      un​≤Can−1​un−1​−an​un​​

      We add all thee inequalities up, getting a nicely telescoping sum,

      i=N+1∑n​ui​≤CaN​uN​​−Can​un​​<CaN​uN​​

      This directly shows up that the tail of the series is bounded by a constant, and therefore the series is convergent for all sufficiently large n.

      Gauss’ Test

      It is an application of Kummer’s Theorem to series where un​>0 when the ratios of successive un​ approaches unity - causing the previous tests to give us indeterminate results.

      If, for large n,

      un+1​un​​=1+nh​+n2B(n)​

      Where B(n) is a bounded function for n sufficiently large, the the Gauss test states that ∑n​un​ converges for h>1 and diverges for h≤1. There is no indeterminate case here.

      This tests is very sensitive, and will work for most of the series that one has to deal with in Physics.

      How does the Gauss test follow from Kummer's theorem?

      Take an​=nln(n). Like we saw before as the last example (Slowly diverging function) in Reducing the requirements on f(x), the series ∑n​an−1​ diverges very slowly - slower than the Harmonic series. This makes it a good candidate from the Kummer test, as we mentioned before. Taking a limit on the left side of the first inequality in Kummer’s theorem,

      n→∞lim​[nln(n)(1+nh​+n2B(n)​)−(n+1)ln(n+1)]=n→∞lim​[−(n+1)ln(nn+1​)+(h−1)ln(n)]

      As we can see, for h<1 both the terms are negative - signalling a diverging case as per Kummer’s theorem. In the case that h>1, the second term will dominate the first (negative) term - making the limit positive, and signalling convergence. If h=1, we are only left with the negative term, indicating divergence.

      Gauss' test with the Legendre Series

      The Legendre equation has terms that follow the recursion relation/ratio,

      a2j​a2j+2​​=(2j+1)(2j+2)2j(2j+1)−λ​

      We may define uj​=a2j​ to tidy up the indices. Then,

      uj+1​uj​​=2j(2j+1)−λ(2j+1)(2j+2)​

      If j is large, we can ignore the λ term (we don’t really just ignore it - just realise that it is bounded), and write,

      uj+1​uj​​→2j2j+2​+j2B(j)​=1+j1​+j2B(j)​

      here, h=1, and the Gauss test tells us that the series is divergent (as expected for Legendre Series, and the reason why we demand a termination of the series).

      Alternating Series

      In comparison with series that consist entirely of positive terms, alternating series have terms that alternate in sign. The partial cancellation due to this sign alteration causes the sum to converge more rapidly, and makes identification of the convergence much easier.

      Remember that for series with even more irregular sign alterations, Cauchy (or Maclaurin) Integral Test’s modified versions are very useful too.

      Leibniz criterion

      If we have a series of the form,

      n=1∑∞​(−1)n+1an​

      with an​>0, then if an​ is monotonically decreasing (for sufficiently large n) and limn→∞​an​=0, then the series converges.

      Proof of Leibniz Criterion

      After the partial sum s2n​, the remainder sum R2n​ maybe written as,

      R2n​=(a2n+1​−an+2​)+(a2n+3​−a2n+4​)+⋯=a2n+1​−(a2n+2​−a2n+3​)−(a2n+4​−a2n+5​)−…

      Remembering the fact that an​s are decreasing, the first equality tells us that R2n​>0 while the second equality tells us that R2n​<a2n+1​. Now, the remainder is thus positive and bounded - which means that we can arbitrarily “tighten” the bound by taking larger and larger n. This means convergence.
