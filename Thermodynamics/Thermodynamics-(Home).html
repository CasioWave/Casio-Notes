What is Thermodynamics?

Thermodynamics is the study of the restrictions on the possible properties of matter that follow from the symmetry properties of the fundamental laws of physics.

When we attempt to describe matter, we generally specify some easily quantifiable (i.e. something that may be compared to a standard easily) macroscopic quantity. This may be:

  • Mass/Weight
  • Position
  • Velocity/Momentum And so on.

If we can decompose the system that we are describing into a collection of particles, then, classically speaking, a full characterization of the system can be achieved if we can simply state for all the particles in the system. Using the laws of mechanics, everything else can be found out from this. For more complicated systems, we might require an effective description of their internal states as well.

So why do the macroscopic quantities that we so commonly use work as a good substitute for this detailed description of the system? This is due to two fundamental characteristics of macroscopic measurement:

  1. Extremely slow: Macroscopic measurements are extremely slow if compared to the timescales of atoms (or equivalent particles).
  2. Extremely coarse: Macroscopic measurements are by definition very “coarse”, i.e they are carried out on lengthscales that are much larger than the atomic/particulate lengthscales.

These measurements simply can not extract the full details of the system - they instead give us a value that is averaged over both length and time!

Macroscopic observables are thus represented by the few quantities that can be derived from the full microscopic details of the system which also happen to be time and space independent on the scales of macroscopic measurement. Thermodynamics is concerned with the study of such quantities.

”Hidden” Modes

Of course, from the previous discussion it is clear that the system may also have modes that we can not resolve using macroscopic measurement. These modes may also carry energy, and thus must be accounted for to have a meaningful analysis of the behaviour of the system.

Qualitative description: Heat

A transfer of energy via these “hidden” modes of the system is called heat.

Simple Systems

In all preliminary analysis, it will be assumed that these thermodynamic systems are simple - either purely mechanical or electromagnetic. This is entirely in the interests of exposition, and does not demonstrate a bound on the applicability of thermodynamics. Consider a simple system to be a system which satisfies the following properties:

  1. Macroscopically homogenous
  2. Isotropic
  3. Uncharged
  4. Large enough to neglect surface effects
  5. Not acted upon by gravitational or electromagnetic field

Conservation Principles

Conservation of energy

Energy is conserved - this is equivalent to the statement “The laws of physics are independent of time”.

Based on the conservation of energy, we are led to believe that macroscopic systems also have definite and precise energies. These energies must thus also be subject to the conservation principle.

Since we can only define the energy of a system with respect to a certain state (i.e. the value of energy of a system is always relative), we always first define a fiducial state and define it to have zero energy.

Equilibrium States

In all systems, there is a tendency to evolve towards states in which the properties of the system are defined by the intrinsic factors of the system, and not by the previously applied external influences on the state.

Equilibrium States

These simple, terminal states which are by definition independent, where the properties of the system are determined solely by the intrinsic factors of the system are called equilibrium states.
