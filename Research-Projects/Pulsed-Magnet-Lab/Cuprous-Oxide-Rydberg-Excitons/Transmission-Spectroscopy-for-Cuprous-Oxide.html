What are the key optical and instrumental parameters required to design and optimise a transmission spectroscopy setup for detecting Rydberg Excitons in Cuprous Oxide?

Of course, we require the following:

  1. High Spectral resolution
  2. Cryogenic (<4K) operation
  3. Highly controlled optical power
  4. High quality single crystal sample with a small enough (how much?) thickness
  5. Strain-free mounting
  6. Precise temperature control

The resolution requirement depends upon the target principal quantum number. I do not know how far down we can go with our monochromator/grating - but it should definitely be less than for the Yellow-Series 1. Cryogenic operation, below 4K is also crucial 1.

There is a requirement that the optical power of the exciting laser be carefully controlled for the experiment to be successful. This essentially reduces the population of excitons and can be adjusted so that exciton-exciton interactions can be ignored 2.

Relevant paper for absorption spectroscopy of Cuprous Oxide

Link: https://doi.org/10.1088/1367-2630/ad8c74 Title: Microwave-optical spectroscopy of Rydberg excitons in the ultrastrong driving regime. Why is it relevant? The paper describes an experiment where they drive Cuprous Oxide sample into the “Ultrastrong” regime. This essentially translates to a high quantum number target for the Rydberg excitons observed in the experiment. Discussed more here: Microwave-optical spectroscopy of Rydberg excitons in the ultrastrong driving regine, Brewin et al

Optimal parameters for such optical absorption spectroscopy mentioned in 2 are:

  • Light source centered around 571-580 nm
  • Focusing optics of Numerical Aperture 0.42-0.6

Natural single crystals generally outperform synthetic samples, with n=12-25 states being observed in the former 2. In synthetic samples, n=7-10 is normally observable 3. This is primarily due to lower defect densities and reduced copper vacancy concentrations in the natural samples 3.

4 describes an experiment carried out with a natural crystal that was mounted on a transparent substrate and oriented such that the 111 crystal plane was parallel to the surface. It was mechanically polished to ~ . A 2x3 mm rectangle of this sample was mounted on a 5 mm diameter CaF2 window. Experimental details are present in 5. Note that strain-free mounting is very important (Prevents SHG generation in bulk due to destruction of inversion symmetry).

Thus, the most important factor in being able to observe higher-n states of excitons is the sample quality rather than the instrumental capability. Above n=20, the electron-hole plasma and charged impurities quench the states 6.

The states here are spaced as , and thus spectral resolution requirements scale with target quantum number. It hits around beyond n=20.

Detection accuracy is greatly enhanced by finer temperature control and coherent transfer matrix analysis methods 6. (Read up on this. Coherent Transfer Matrix analysis)

Survey of observed states for yellow-series Rydberg excitons in literature

Most experimental studies detected states between n=7 and n=13. Cryogenic temperatures (below 150K) were used in all, with the lowest temperatures used being 1.2K.

Transmission Spectroscopy Instrumental Configurations

Spectrometer and Detection Systems

There were a variety of spectrometer configurations used across the studies reviewed. Mostly, the typical grating monochromators of resolutions between were used, with some even surpassing this range.

Case Study Spectrometer: Princeton Instruments HRS-750

  • 1200 gratings/mm
  • slit With these settings, a 0.036 nm resolution was achieved in 7.

Some experiments like 1 used double monochromator setup for both pre- and post-sample spectral filtering.

Detection was done using Si based CCDs and Photodiodes. 2 Even used temperature-tuned Fabry-Perot etalons for sideband extraction in their microwave-modulated experiments - possibly entirely irrelevant to our current experiment.

Light Sources and Optical Configuration

Broadband light sources were most widely used, with LED sources centered at 580nm with 14nm width. 7 Describes the following setup:

The excitation light from a broadband white light source (Thorlabs SLS201L) was focused onto the sample with an objective lens with NA = 0.42 (Mitutoyo Plan Apo 20x)

Other (narrowband) sources I encountered are:

  • 2 Frequency-doubled lasers at approximately 571nm
  • 1 Tunable dye lasers

Optical path typically had focusing optics with the NA values previously mentioned for both sample illumination and light collection. Spot sizes achieved were ~ FWHM. Aspheric lenses with higher NA were used to focus the beams to much smaller (~ ) diameter 4.

Specialized Optical Components

There were some setups like 4 that utilized 785-nm long-pass dichroic mirrors, 1000-nm short-pass filters, and bandpass filters centered at 580nm. Polarization control was achieved in 1 through linear polarizers that had specific orientations with respect to the crystal axes. (Noise eaters were used in the tunable dye laser setup, to reduce laser intensity fluctuations by a factor of 200. What are these?)

Critical Optical Parameters

Wavelength and Spectral Resolution

Yellow series Rydberg exciton detection requires excitation light to be in the range . Higher spectral resolution is a must to observe higher-n states because of the close spacing.

Optical Power and Intensity

Powers ranged from 2 to 2 mW. Several studies used for their temperature dependent measurements. I assume this is heavily dependent on the sample dimensions, though. Low power lasers () were used to avoid depolarization effects 1. (It is assumed that depolarization here refers to a loss of coherence in the system. Maybe low power avoids driving the system into non-linear regimes)

Beam Geometry and Focusing

Focusing of the beam significantly impacted the detection performance of the experiments, as reported. Tight focusing was achieved with the NA values mentioned before. Spatial non-uniformity and broader excitation spot sizes where noted as factors that were detrimental to measurement quality.

Polarization Control

Useful for selective excitation of exciton states. Linear polarization with respect to crystal axes (i.e. [100], [010], [001], [110]) optimized the detection (why?). Excitation light polarization and angular structure strongly influenced the nonlinear optical response (why?).

Sample Requirements and Preparation

Crystal Quality and Structure

As stated before, natural crystals outperformed synthetic samples when compared on the basis of high-n state detection. (Do we have any X-Ray crystallographic data on our sample? All experiments reviewed had a single crystal).

Single crystals were preferred over polycrystalline samples to minimize strains. Cuts around crystal planes were done - but I am not entirely sure what that achieves.

Sample thickness and Geometry

Thickness varied from to depending on the preparation method. Natural crystals were mechanically polished. Synthetic films were much larger. The thickness of the sample is important - it must be measured accurately. This, and “wedge effects” influence interference phenomena, and must be considered during data analysis.

Purity and Defect Considerations

High purity and low defect density are of course essential for the detection of high-n states. Synthetic samples apparently show a significant excess of copper vacancies as compared to natural crystals. This affects spatial broadening, and spatial inhomogeneity.

Surface Quality and Mounting

Surface quality requirements included:

  • Smooth surface: Minimizes interference effects
  • Approximately flat and parallel (to beam path): Optimal transmission, lower reflective losses. Often, CaF2 windows were often the substrate, put onto copper mounts.

Environmental and Experimental Conditions

Temperature Control

Cryogenic temperatures are universally required, as expected. 4K was common. Somewhat resolvable spectra was observed upto 150K.

Cooling

Here, we just use what is available.

Vibration Isolation and Shielding

Vibrations from the refrigerator should be avoided, and low-vibration setups are obviously preferred. Kapton spacers and Quartz plates are used to minimize mechanical stress.

The microwave modulated experiment required some thought regarding the antenna frequency responses of the setup.

Vacuum and Atmospheric Conditions

Vacuum environments were implied by the use of closed-cycle refrigerators and cryostats. Vacuums also reduce thermal noise significantly.

Detection Performance

The range of n-states that were observed is described before. Note that the linewidth broadening was state-dependent, with a FWHM of 14 GHz observed for 8p states. Linewidths may deviate from the expected scaling in some synthetic samples due to interactions with the background electron-hole plasma.

p- excitons have a much higher (2 orders of magnitude) intensities as compared to other spectral lines. Excitons and polariton states are distinguishable given good enough signal strength and resolution. Did not find too much on optimizing SNR.

Recommended Optimizations from survey

  • Spectral resolution is important - push it as far as possible without trading SNR.
  • Polarization control is useful to have. It can help distinguish between rydberg exciton states.
  • Temperature control should be good, to help with temperature-resolution and figure out how the spectra depends on it.
  • Low power excition lowers depolarization effects, and exciton-exciton interactions. but this reduces signal strength. This must be optimized (is there a straightforward way to do this?)
  • Thinner samples have enhanced interference effects.
  • Coherent transfer matrix method is a much more accurate analysis as compared to just Beer’s Law, especially for high-n states.
  • Elliot’s model was used to fit temperature dependent data and optimize detection parameters. (Look into how this was done)
  • Coherence of beam is important, and should be on the scale of the experiment.
  • Noise eaters can help, if there are intensity fluctuations from the excitation optics for some reason.
