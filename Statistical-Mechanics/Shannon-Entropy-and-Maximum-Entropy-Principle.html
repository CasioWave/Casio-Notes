Quantifying Uncertainty: Shannon Entropy

The concept of “entropy” in statistical mechanics is fundamentally related to the idea of “missing information” from information theory. Claude Shannon (They named the AI after him) figured out a way to quantify this missing information in a mathematically precise way.

We start with the intuitive idea of how “surprising” an event is. If an event is very likely, we are not surprised when it occurs. If it is very unlikely, we are surprised when it occurs. The Principle of Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt)

Now that we can quantify missing information, we come back to the original problem. Suppose that we have a physical system (make it simple, and assume a box with some gas in it). The system can be in one of a vast number of microstates .

Microstate

A state of the system defined by a specification of all the microscopic details, i.e. a point in the phase space of the system.

Typically, we do not know the exact microstate that the system resides in at any given moment. Instead, we have access to a few macroscopic properties which are averages over the microstates of the system.

Principle of Maximum Entropy

We should use a probability distribution that maximizes the Gibbs entropy of the system, subject to the constraints of our knowledge of the system through the macroscopic properties that we can measure.

This is obviously the right choice to make - any other choice of a probability distribution implies that we have more information than we actually have access to! By maximizing the entropy, we essentially are choosing a distribution that is maximally non-committal about the information that we do not have. It is the most honest thing to do.
