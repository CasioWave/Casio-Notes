      The Schrodinger Equation

      The Schrodinger Equation

      Mar 18, 20254 min read

      • Quantum
      • QM
      • Schrodinger

      In Classical Mechanics, we are used to describing the motion of a particle using a time dependent 3D vector - x(t) as the dynamical variable.

      In Quantum Theory and related wave mechanics, the dynamical variable is instead a wavefunction.

      Naive definition of a wavefunction

      A wavefunction is generally a complex valued function whose value depends upon both time and space. When all 3 dimensions of space are relevant, we may express such a wavefunction by:

      Ψ(x,t)∈C

      If there is just one relevant space dimenson, we may write:

      Ψ(x,t)∈C

      A valid wavefunction in Quantum Mechanics is characterized by the fact that it is solution to the Schrodinger Equation. We may write the Schrodinger equation as:

      iℏ∂t∂Ψ​(x,t)=(−2mℏ2​∂x2∂2​+V(x,t))Ψ(x,t) , For 1 dimension iℏ∂t∂Ψ​(x,t)=(−2mℏ2​∇2+V(x,t))Ψ(x,t) , For 3 dimensions

      The equations describe a particle (non-relativistic) of mass m moving (along the x-axis, for the 1D case) while acted upon by the potential V(x,t)∈R.

      Note that the wavefunction is necessarily complex! If it was real, then the right-hand-side of the SE would be entirely real while the left-hand-side of the SE would be completely imaginary - something that can not happen.

      We make the following remarks:

      1. The Schrodinger equation is a First-oder differential equation in time.
        • This means that if we specify a wavefunction Ψ(x,t0​), then the wavefunction is determined ∀t.
      2. The Schrodinger equation is a linear equation in Ψ, i.e. if Ψ1​ and Ψ2​ are solutions to the SE, then Ψ1​+Ψ2​ is also a valid solution of the SE.

      Probability (Born interpretation)

      We can define a probability density for a valid wavefunction, as follows:

      P(x,t)≡Ψ∗(x,t)Ψ(x,t)=∣Ψ(x,t)∣2

      obviously, the definition satissfies the requirement that the PDF be positive definite. We may also interpret this PDF in the following way:

      Interpretation of the PDF of a wavefunction

      P(x,t)dx is the Probability to find the particle represented by the wavefunction in the interval [x,x+dx] at some time t.

      Of course, if we interpret the PDF this way, it must be normalizable to unity as required by the Axioms of Probability. This is given by:

      ∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx=1,∀t

      Is normalization of the wavefunction preserved under time-evolution of the wavefunction?

      Suppose that we have a wavefunction Ψ that has been normalized at some time t0​:

      ∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t0​)∣2dx=1

      We have previously claimed that if a valid wavefunction is specified for some time t0​, it is specfied ∀t. In addition to this, if the interpretation of the probability density defined is to hold, we have to verify that the Schroddinger equation guarantees that the wavefunction remains normalized at all times.

      • See: SE preserves normalization (Proof)

      Probability current

      It is interesting to note that a form that shows up during the proof, namely:

      J(x,t)=mℏ​Im(Ψ∗∂x∂Ψ​)

      is defined as the Probability current associated with the wavefunction. It is useful to characterise the flow of probability. As it is a current of a conserved quantity (Probability), it clearly has a conservation equation:

      ∇⋅J+∂t∂ρ​=0
      • See: Conservation of probability current associated with wavefunction (Proof)

      This quantity is often very useful. For example, let us define Pab​(t) as the probability of finding the particle represented by Ψ(x,t) in the interval [a,b]:

      Pab​(t)=∫ab​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2d=∫ab​ρ(x,t)dx

      Using the definition of the probability current here, w can easily show that:

      dtdPab​(t)​=J(a,t)−J(b,t)

      which is an equation hich looks quite like a master equation, with terms for inflow and outflow of probabilities on the right-hand-side.

      Physically equivalent wavefunctions

      Sometimes, it is easier to deal with wavefunctions that are not normalized. In this spirit, we may defined that two wavefunctions Ψ1​ and Ψ2​ are physically equivalent if they differ by multiplication with some complex number. We normally write this as:

      Ψ1​∼Ψ2​↔Ψ1​(x,t)=αΨ2​(x,t),α∈C

      Note: If Ψ1​ and Ψ2​ are both normalized, then they must differ by multiplication with some overall constant phase factor, i.e. eiθ,θ∈R.
