      Schrodinger Equation preserves the normalization of a wavefunction

      Schrodinger Equation preserves the normalization of a wavefunction

      Mar 18, 20253 min read

      • Quantum
      • Proof

      Claim

      Given a valid 1D wavefunction Ψ(x,t) (i.e. a solution to the Schrodinger equation) that is normalized to unity at some time t0​,

      ∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t0​)∣2dx=1

      we claim that the wavefunction remains normalized to unity ∀t,

      ∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx=1,∀t

      Proof

      We alter the claim slightly to claim that given the initial normalization, we have:

      dtd​∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx=0

      Now, we know that:

      ∣Ψ(x,t)∣2=Ψ∗(x,t)Ψ(x,t)

      Plugging this in the LHS of the claimed equality, and interchanging the integral with the derivative (as they are carried with respect to different independent variables):

      dtd​∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx⟹∫−∞∞​(∂t∂Ψ∗​Ψ+∂t∂Ψ​Ψ∗)dx​

      Now, we can see that this equation has time derivatives of the wavefunction and its complex conjugate - which means that we can substitute the SE here. First, we determine the Complex conjugate of the SE:

      −iℏ∂t∂Ψ∗​(x,t)=(−2mℏ2​∂x2∂2​+V(x,t))Ψ∗(x,t)

      Putting this in the previous expression, we get:

      ⟹ℏi​∫−∞∞​((−2mℏ2​∂x2∂2​+V)Ψ∗Ψ+(2mℏ2​∂x2∂2​−V)Ψ∗Ψ)dx

      The V terms neatly cancel out, as they only act via multiplication on the wavefunction. After some cleanup, we get:

      ⟹∫−∞∞​∂x∂​[2miℏ​(Ψ∗∂x∂Ψ​−∂x∂Ψ∗​Ψ)]dx

      So, the intergal ends up simplifying to:

      2miℏ​[Ψ∗∂x∂Ψ​−∂x∂Ψ∗​Ψ]−∞∞​

      We make two very crucial assumptions, so that this expression neatly goes to zero:

      1. The wavefunction dies off at ±∞, i.e.: ∣Ψ(x,t)∣→0 as ∣x∣→±∞. This ensures that the wavefunction remains square-normalizable over the entire domain.
      2. ∂x∂Ψ​is bounded as ∣x∣→±∞. This is a more general demand than asking the derivative to go to zero at ±∞. Under these two assumptions, the expression goes to zero. Therefore, we can say:
      dtd​∫−∞∞​∣Ψ(x,t)∣2dx=0 ( Proved :) )
